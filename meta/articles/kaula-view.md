With these words, we arrived at our destination: the residence of Padma Prasad Bhatta, the initiating priest (dīkṣā-pūjārī) of the Paśupatinātha Temple, a man entrusted with the authority to uphold the lineage of the Paśupatinātha Temple priests (pūjārīs) (Fig. 24). Padma Prasad not only possesses an encyclopedic knowledge of Nepali paddhatis and tantras, but is also widely recognized as an accomplished sādhaka of the Kāpālika, Śrīvidyā, and Guhyakālī Kaula traditions. During our two-hour conversation, he revealed many important aspects of the interconnections among various tantric communities in Nepal and also discussed the tantric practices that transform the human body into what he called the 'all-powerful body of Śiva-Śakti,' the abode of all the gods. Padma Prasad explained:

> 'In Tantra, the gods do not have an external abode. They dwell within the centers of the body. We generate them internally and then install them in external images. Afterwards, we reabsorb them into ourselves. In this way, we perfect and empower our body, and thus we become the Absolute. This is the essence of Tantra.'

Timalsina later commented on Padma Prasad's statement:

> 'The statement that "the gods do not have an external abode" is excellent. Only a Kaulin like Padmaji could know such things. People either naively think that the gods live in some other world, or they are skeptical and assume that the gods do not exist. But the gods certainly do exist. The gods are the forms of the syllables, and the syllables are the basis of creation. Yet this creation is our creation. There is no other who created us. We create ourselves as everything that appears before us. We place creation within ourselves as ourselves. And so, in our daily pūjās and meditative visualizations, we manifest the powers of Tripurasundarī to project creation outward from within Herself, constructing the gods through our mantra recitation, installing them in images, and then reabsorbing them into ourselves at the conclusion of our practice. In this way, just like, or rather, as Tripurasundarī Herself, we create, sustain, conceal, destroy, and liberate universes.'
