## Spread
Comparable would be Karuppusvāmī (with a paurāņika attestation in the form of Mahākāla the Dvārapāla of Śiva) with a Pan-Jambudvīpīya presence in TN, KL, Lanka, Singapore, Malaysia and recently a following in Gujarat under the name of Ekalvīr dādā.

Karuppu has found a following in Gujarat under the name, “Ekalvīr Dādā”. Some GJ Hindus even leave comments of gratitude & love for tamizh YouTube videos of Karuppu devotional songs!

## sadAchAra
One of the reasons why Karuppusvāmīs at several places are manifesting a more “transcendental inner layer” and adopting what is generally termed as sadācāra is likely that many of his devotees are approaching Karuppu through Ayyappa’s (not Śāstā or Ayyanār in general but the Naișțhika Ayyappa of Sabarimalai) prism. 

Something that many do not realize is that Ayyappa is bringing many groups of people (known for their rough way of life & communal background) closer than ever to sat-sampradāyas & sadācāra. Just like he tames the lesser Bhūtas and brings them within his firm control, He is doing that with humans here as well. 

And to those who have received his grace, deities like Karuppu are manifesting their inner layers as well.

## mahA-kAla
Source: [TW](https://x.com/GhorAngirasa/status/2054922895131099425)

Karuppasvāmī is an amazing deity of ferocious power (matched only by his profound sense of justice and vatsala for Bhaktas and those in distress) and He does not play by any of our rules here. 

There are places where He has graciously—through the sovereign will of Śiva—allowed himself to be worshiped with Āgamika rites by Āgamika priests.

There are other places where He remains in his primal form and is worshiped with liquor, meat and beedi by his traditional priests. 

Both places with their different priesthoods and different ritual systems coexist. 

He has been worshiped in the latter mode for centuries prior to any Dravidianist movement. 

Advanced Upāsakas of Śāstā know him as a rural/folk form of Mahākāla, one of the two main doorkeepers of Śiva/Rudra. 

And the Vāyavīya Saṁhitā of the Śivapurāṇa (which is a pro-Āgamika and orthodox text) expressly states that Mahākāla is fond of meat and liquor and praises him as a great punisher of the wicked. 

If one knows where to look and what to look for, one can find things that he or she is uncomfortable with in the Veda and other “mainstream” texts.
