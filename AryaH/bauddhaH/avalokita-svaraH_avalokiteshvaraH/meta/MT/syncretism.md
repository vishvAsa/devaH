The transformation of the name of this bodhisattva is an interesting case of syncretizing lateral H influence on the bauddha. It is interesting to see how it is reflected in chIna translations. The great earlier translator kumArajIva tends to render it as: Guān-shì-yīn (觀世音), which was supposed to capture: "the one who perceives the world's laments." This is congruent with avalokitasvara appearing in the manuscripts written in a register of Skt from 400-500s in Turkestan. 

However, the chIna scholar Xuanzang, who traveled to India, translated the name as Guān-zì-zài (觀自在), which means "the lord who perceives/gazes" – this is equivalent to avalokiteshvara. This means that between 500s-600s, the name underwent a transformation in India, most likely under shaiva influence (hence, Ishvara). This probably happened in a community of syncretic practitioners who had simultaneous bauddha and shaiva affinities.
